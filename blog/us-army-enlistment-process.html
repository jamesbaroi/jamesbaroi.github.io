U.S. Army Enlistment Process
J. A. Baroi
2023-07-16
Overview
You are considering enlisting in the United States Army and are preparing to meet with your local recruiter for the first time. You may be wondering, "What is the enlistment process really like?" Recognizing that there are many articles available on this subject that enumerate the general facts of enlistment, in this article I will share more nuanced information and provide suggestions, based on my own experience, to afford you more detail about enlisting in the Army as an active-duty soldier, and forgo much of the surface information that is easily accessable elsewhere. Army recruitment is a nation wide endeavor and is continuously changing. Therefore, some parts of the enlistment process may be different for you based on where you are located and when you may be reading this.

Introduction
The U.S. Army enlistment program is governed by Army Regulation AR 601-210, which prescribes the official eligibility criteria, policies, and procedures for enlistment. In order to determine eligibility, your recruiter will have you go through a series of steps which will involve: information gathering and documentation, aptitude testing, background check, medical and physical evaluation, and job selection. Once the process is complete and you have been deemed eligible, you may sign your enlistment contract and prepare to ship out to basic combat training (BCT). The entire process may take as little as a week or as long as a few months or more, depending on your particular situation.
Preliminary Research
The first step in the enlistment process is not, as so many other soruces would suggest, to speak to a recruiter, but to do your homework. Research the Army to determine if this is the right path for you. Popular sources of information include army.mil, the official homepage of the U.S. Army, and goarmy.com, where you can find information about the military occupational specialties (MOS) or jobs that the Army provides, one of which you must ultimately select during the enlistment process.
While conducting your research, jot down questions that you may have for your recruiter for when you meet. This will save both of you time and also impress upon your recruiter that you are a serious candidate. In turn, it may motivate your recruiter to prioritize your application over others'.
Note that during the research phase of the process you may discover the difference between an enlisted soldier and a commissioned officer. If you are interested in joining as an officer, explore the options that are available and the corresponding eligibility criteria as described in the appropriate regulations listed in the Army Publishing Directorate (APD). Inquire into the process with your recruiter. I will continue to focus on active-duty enlistment since that is the experience I have and to which I can attest.
Finally, be advised that the Army is a massive organization, and the information available about it is equally extensive. Do not get bogged down in research. Rather, focus on what specifically interests you and concerns that you may have. A good rule of thumb may be to conduct research up to the point where you have either decided that you do not, in fact, want to enlist, or jotted down as many questions as may occur to you to ask your recruiter. Compile a list of the types of questions to which the answers will clarify your decision to join.
Meeting with Your Recruiter
After conducting preliminary research, contact your local recruiter and set up an appointment. Show up ten minutes early and in business casual attire. Be mindful of personal appearance, grooming, and hygiene before showing up. If in doubt, reference the appearance and grooming policies as prescribed by Army Regulation AR 670-1, specifically the relevant sections in Chapter 3. While it is not necessary to hold yourself to the standards of the Army just yet, you may as well get a head start and further impress your recruiter with a solid first impression. Remember to bring your State issued driver's license or identification card, social security card, and birth certificate as instructed by your recruiter.
The Q & A
The first part of your meeting will likely involve your recruiter asking you some basic questions about yourself (similar to questions that may have been asked in your initial phone call); questions about your age, citizenship, marital status, number of dependents if any, current living situation, employment status, level of education, health conditions, criminal history and drug use, financial obligations (if you have any debts for example), travels and ties to foreign countries, your motivations for joining the Army, and so on. Answer all questions honestly. Keep your answers short and to the point. Avoid an exposition of your entire life. For example, when being asked about your health, stick to addressing any major surgeries or illnesses for which you were admitted to a hospital or clinic and for which medical records exist. There is no need to go into every minor cut or bruise that you have ever sustained. The recruiter is looking to eliminate any potential red flags that may show up on a search for documented records that may disqualify you for enlistment. Keep in mind that it is in the recruiter's best interest that you enlist and so you are well advised not to volunteer any unnecesary information that may hinder that goal.
The Hard Sell
Having gained a sense of your situation, the recruiter is likely to spend some time elaborating on the benefits the Army provides; benefits such as a steady and immediate source of stable income, effectively free room and board for single soldiers or housing and subsistance (food) allowances for married soldiers, various sign-up bonuses pertaining to specific contractual agreements (for example, sign-up bonuses for particular jobs), the possibility for travel to exotic places and being stationed in a variety of different States and countries, the numerous facilities and outlets available on Army bases, the many possibilites for career advancement such as attending special courses (for example, Airborne), financial assistance for education such as the Tuition Assistance (TA) Program and the Loan Repayment Program (LRP), benefits of the GI Bill, the Credentialing Opportunities On-Line (COOL) tailored to specific jobs, health and medical benefits not just for you but also your family (spouse, and children), assistance with loans for buying a house, retirement and savings options, and much more.
This part of the meeting is often likened to a sales pitch where the recruiter is perceived to "sell you" on the Army, and may involve misunderstanding that the recruiter is exaggerating or misrepresenting the benefits of joining the Army in some way simply to convince you to sign on the dotted line. While there may very well be some recruiters who resort to dubious sales tactics (mine did not), understand that you are free to interrogate the recruiter to clarify the finer points of their claims and that it is ultimately your responsibility to verify the details of their assertions. This is where your preliminary research and list of prepared questions come in handy. Be mindful to continue your research throughout the enlistment process as new information arise.
The Fine Print
While the Army does indeed provide benefits competitive with many civilian jobs, like with any other opportunity it is important to read the fine print not only to determine if you actually do qualify for those benefits that are of particular interest to you, but to understand the steps you may have to take to receive those benefits. For example, if you are interested in the Loan Repayment Program, which currently offers non-prior, active-duty soldiers, in certain jobs, up to a total of $65,000 (less taxes) in repayment towards certain types of student loans, you will have to decline enrollment in the Montgomery GI Bill (MGIB) in writing included in your first enlistment contract. Had you not known of this crucial step and simply waited after signing your enlistment contract before delving into the details, you would have missed the opportunity altogether.
A general rule of thumb to receiving benefits from the Army is that your chances of doing so are maximized if you read the fine print at the time of constructing your enlistment contract (for such benefits as can be guaranteed on the contract) because this is the time the Army is willing to offer you as much as possible in return for your commitment. Once you are committed - which is to say, once you have signed your contract - the Army has no further incentive to fulfill your desires beyond its own needs and standing obligations.
The Mock Test
After you have had your conversation with the recruiter and are ready to proceed with the enlistment process, the next step on your first meeting will be to take a mock aptitude test to get a sense of how you will fare in the actual test. This will be a shorter test which will take about an hour or less to complete and will include questions similar to the real one. Take the test seriously and do your best but do not stress about the results - they will not count towards anything but the level of preparation that you may have to undertake for when you are facing the real thing. Your recruiter may take this time to make copies of your driver's license or ID, social securty card, and birth cirtificate. Once you complete the test your recruiter will score it then and there and talk with you about the results. If you pass comfortably then your recruiter will want to schedule the actual test as soon as possible. If you score below passing or you just barely pass then your recruiter is likely to recommend that you prepare more thoroughly and encourage you to stick to a study routine in order to pass with a sufficient margin of error (more on this later in the "Armed Services Vocational Aptitude Battery" section below).
The Next Step
Once you have considered a tentative testing date or have committed to a study plan, your recruiter will send you on your way with instructions for the next meeting which will involve completing an information packet and gathering necessary documents and paperwork.
Documents and Paperwork
The information packet that you must fill out to enlist will consist of a more thorough list of similar questions to the ones that your recruiter would have asked you in your first meeting. Completing the packet will require three character references who you have known for the past ten years, as well as references who can verify your place of residence for the past ten years or up to your 16th birthday, and references who can verify your employment for the past ten years or up to your 17th birthday. These references cannot include family members and no refernce can be listed more than once. Information on family members will also be required to include your parents, siblings, spouse, spouse's parents, and children. Getting in touch with potential references and family members ahead of the enlistment process can therefore speed up completing the packet.
While you should be as accurate as possible in the information you provide, do not stress about making mistakes in the packet. The physical packet is a precursor to the information that will be entered by your recruiter into the Army's electronic system. You will have opportunities throughout the process to correct any mistakes. You are well advised to sit with and monitor your recruiter's progress during the data transfer into the system to correct any errors or typographical mistakes in real time.
Besides the packet, six other categories of documents may be required throughout the enlistment process, with some being mandatory while others being circumstantial: identity, educational, medical, marital, financial, and legal. Other more detailed sources of information for these requirements are widely available so I will briefly mention them here. Note that whenever possible, obtain original or official documents and records.
(1) Identity documents include, as previously mentioned, your State issued driver's license or identification card, social security card, and birth cirtificate. Other identity documents may include your passport (if any), your certificate of naturalization (if you are a naturalized citizen) or permanent resident card.
(2) Educational documents include your highschool diploma or GED, college diploma (if any), and college transcripts as well as diplomas and transcripts from any other institution of higher education, and any relevant certifications that you may have.
(3) Medical documents include those that exist in any medical databases such as in hospitals or clinics where you may have had surgeries, or were treated for injuries, infections, or illnesses. Information should include the date you were admitted, the condition for admission, and discharge dispositions.
(4) Marital documents, as the name suggests, include documents related to your marriage(s), namely your marriage license(s), or divorce papers.
(5) Financial documents include a credit report (a credit check will be done automatically with your signed consent), and other potential documents as relevant to your situation (such as bankruptcy or debt records).
(6) Legal documents include court dispositions of any crimes or infractions committed with relevant dates, charges, and dispositions.
These categories of documents are not necessarily exhaustive. You may be required to provide other documents as needed based on your personal situation. While you will have sufficient time to gather documents during the enlistment process, now that you are armed with the knowledge of a tentative list of documents, you can get a head start and shorten the time between meeting with your recruiter for the first time and shipping out to basic training. Getting documents out out of the way early will also free up your time to focus on the other parts of the enlistment process, such as the aptitude test discussed next.
Armed Services Vocational Aptitude Battery
The Armed Services Vocational Aptitude Battery (ASVAB) test is yet another necessary part of the enlistment process for which ample sources of information exist, most notably the official site. The test is administered for two main purposes: to qualify for enlistment which requires attaining a passing score, and to assess aptitude in specific areas of knowledge to qualify for and help select a specific job.
Format and Verification
You may take the paper and pencil (P&P) version of the test, the computer adaptive test (CAT-ASVAB), or the untimed and unproctored prescreening, internet-delivered computer adaptive test (PiCAT). You may take the test at your nearest Military Entrance Processing Station (MEPS), at your recruiter's office, or in the case of the PiCAT, at home at your own convenience, or at your recruiter's office. If you take the PiCAT at your recruiter's office (as I did) or at home, you will have to take a verification test at MEPS within a certain time frame (my recruiter drove me to MEPS the same day for verification). The verification test is a shorter version of the test with some questions being exactly the same as the one you took while a few may be different. For those questions that are exactly the same, answer them exactly as you did on the PiCAT even if you realized at some point that you had answered them incorrectly on the actual test. The verification test is proctored to ensure that you are indeed the same person who took the actual test and that you did not cheat. Therefore, it is in your best interest to match all answers on the verification test to the actual one. Note that some percentage of random test takers will have to take the entire test again instead of the short version (to ensure testing validity). After the verification test is complete you will have to answer a different set of questions related to your personality profile. This set of questions will have no bearing on your enlistment.
Percentile and Composite Scores
The Armed Forces Qualification Test (AFQT) percentile score derived from certain sub-sections of the ASVAB, determines your eligibility to join based on a cutoff threshold. For some, the threshold to enlist in the Army is set at 31 percentile points, and for others at 50 percentile points (at the time of this writing), depending on whether you have a highschool diploma, a GED, or other factors that may be applicable to you and that your recruiter can explain.
Composite scores, often referred to as line scores, are a set of scores derived from a combination of sub-sections. These line scores assess specific areas of strength or weakness to determine your eligibility for specific jobs. If there is a certain job that you are interested in then you are well advised to spend more time in preparing for the sub-section(s) of the test that will contribute to the particular line score(s) needed for that job.
If you wish to rise to the ranks of Warrant Officers in the course of your Army career, know that to do so will also require, in some cases, a minimum line score threshold in the subject area relevant to the desired Warrant Officer MOS (WOMOS). Therefore, it is in your best interest to do well on the ASVAB not only for initial eligibility and job selection but also potentially for some long term career aspirations.
Aptitude and Performance
While the ASVAB is not only a necessary step but a crucial one in determining the course of your potential Army career, and while you may improve your score marginally or perhaps even significantly with study, it is important to keep in mind that the results of the test are but one measure of performance, which reflects your aptitude - a characteristic that is to some extent innate (consider child prodigies or the cognitively encumbered). Neither pride nor shame is as helpful a response to your score as an objective and dispassionate recognition of your abilities at the time. Whether you have passed with flying colors, just barely, or with scores somewhere in between, carry on knowing that there is much more to being a good soldier than what any one metric can tell.
